Who are we?

Many of the features included in the Open edX platform are collaboratively developed by the Open edX community. Therefore, community members take a huge part in the project's continuous improvement.

We are a group of people, from various backgrounds, united by the idea that education should be available to anyone, anywhere, at any time. We span the globe - members work from Colombia, the US, the Ukraine, Australia, Venezuela, India, and many, many more locales. We are of all genders, of all races, and of all types of educational backgrounds. We have many different career paths: educators, programmers, project managers, and more -- and sometimes more than one of these!

You're formally invited to meet everyone in the community. You may wish to check out our community section on openedx.org; this is a helpful resource to bookmark.

In this section, you'll learn more about us and how to join us - we have lots of ways of working together, particularly in working groups, the forums, and on our Slack instance.

Why are we here?

We are here working in the Open edX project because we believe in its mission to provide an accessible education to learners of all backgrounds. Accessible means many things, from being literally accessible (WCAG 2.1 compliant) to being online, flexible, and available at any moment of the day. The Open edX project provides massively scalable learning software technology to education institutions, enterprise, and government organizations. We are here everyday to improve the software and learning experiences to enhance the experience of the learner.

Many of us are here because the platform is open-source.

Open-source software is software with source code that anyone can inspect, modify, and enhance.

"Source code" is the part of software that most computer users don't ever see; it's the code computer programmers can manipulate to change how a piece of software—a "program" or "application"—works. Programmers who have access to a computer program's source code can improve that program by adding features to it or fixing parts that don't always work correctly.

By design, open-source software licenses promote collaboration and sharing because they permit other people to make modifications to source code and incorporate those changes into their own projects. They encourage computer programmers to access, view, and modify open-source software whenever they like, as long as they let others do the same when they share their work.

- https://opensource.com/resources/what-open-source

The open-source philosophy and ethos infuses the project with a strong sense of collaboration and sharing. We strive to do "development in public", that is, that all contributors to the project are doing as much of their work as possible in a public fashion, using public collaboration tools like GitHub Projects and our Confluence wiki.

If you're curious about open-source and want to know more, we recommend taking this open-source master class. Some members of our community contributed to the authoring of the course, and we think you'll enjoy it.

What do we do?

This course aims to tell you about all that we in the Open edX community do. We do a lot! We build software to deliver world class learning experiences. We market the platform, to let as many people as possible learn about the Open edX project and begin using it themselves. We translate the software into 20+ languages. We make this course, and courses like it, to help onboard people to the community. We are constantly on the lookout for new community members and extend them a helping hand, to make joining the community as easy and fun as possible.
